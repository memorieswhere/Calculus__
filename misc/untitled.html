Calculus

Calculus, originally called infinitesimal calculus or "the calculus of infinitesimals", is the mathematical study of continuous change, in the same way that geometry is the study of shape, and algebra is the study of generalizations of arithmetic operations. It has two major branches, differential calculus and integral calculus; differential calculus concerns instantaneous rates of change, and the slopes of curves, while integral calculus concerns accumulation of quantities, and areas under or between curves. As the names suggest, differential calculus is simply the study of derivatives of functions, while integral calculus is the study of integrals.

Applications

Calculus is used in every branch of the physical sciences, actuarial science, computer science, statistics, engineering, economics, business, medicine, demography, and in other fields wherever a problem can be mathematically modeled and an optimal solution is desired. It allows one to go from (non-constant) rates of change to the total change or vice versa, and many times in studying a problem we know one and are trying to find the other.
Physics makes particular use of calculus; all concepts in classical mechanics and electromagnetism are related through calculus. The mass of an object of known density, the moment of inertia of objects, and the potential energies due to gravitational and electromagnetic forces can all be found by the use of calculus. An example of the use of calculus in mechanics is Newton's second law of motion, which states that the derivative of an object's momentum with respect to time equals the net force upon it. Alternatively, Newton's second law can be expressed by saying that the net force is equal to the object's mass times its acceleration, which is the time derivative of velocity and thus the second time derivative of spatial position. Starting from knowing how an object is accelerating, we use calculus to derive its path.
Maxwell's theory of electromagnetism and Einstein's theory of general relativity are also expressed in the language of differential calculus. Chemistry also uses calculus in determining reaction rates and in studying radioactive decay. In biology, population dynamics starts with reproduction and death rates to model population changes.
Green's theorem, which gives the relationship between a line integral around a simple closed curve C and a double integral over the plane region D bounded by C, is applied in an instrument known as a planimeter, which is used to calculate the area of a flat surface on a drawing. For example, it can be used to calculate the amount of area taken up by an irregularly shaped flower bed or swimming pool when designing the layout of a piece of property.
In the realm of medicine, calculus can be used to find the optimal branching angle of a blood vessel so as to maximize flow.Calculus can be applied to understand how quickly a drug is eliminated from a body or how quickly a cancerous tumour grows.
In economics, calculus allows for the determination of maximal profit by providing a way to easily calculate both marginal cost and marginal revenue.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Recommended reading: Derivatives, Integrals
The fundamental theorem of calculus is a theorem that links the concept of differentiating a function (calculating the gradient) with the concept of integrating a function (calculating the area under the curve). It establishes that differentiation and integration have an inverse relationship, in other words, they are inverse operations of each other.

First Part

If \(\color{black}f\) be a continuous real-valued function defined on a closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\), then the function defined as \(\displaystyle F(x) = \int_{a}^x f(t)\ dt\) is continuous on \(\color{black}[a, b]\) and differentiable on the open interval \(\color{black}(a, b)\), and \(\displaystyle F'(x) = f(x)\) for all \(\color{black}x\) in \(\color{black}(a,b)\) so \(\color{black}F\) is an antiderivative of \(\color{black}f\)

Second Part

The Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus relates definite integrals with differentiation and provides a method to compute a definite integral when the antiderivative of its integrand is known. Let \(\color{black}f\) be a real-valued function on a closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\) and \(\color{black}F\) a continuous function on \(\color{black}[a, b]\), which is an antiderivative of \(\color{black}f\), such that \(\displaystyle \color{black}F'(x) = f(x) \quad \text{ or } \quad F(x) = \int f(x) + C\) If \(\color{black}f\) is Riemann-integrable on \(\color{black}[a, b]\), then \(\displaystyle \int_{a}^b f(x)\ dx = F(b) - F(a)\) stating that the integral of \(\color{black}f\) over the interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\) is equal to [the antiderivative evaluated at \(\color{black}b\)] - [the antiderivative evaluated at \(\color{black}a\)]. The theorem can also be equivalently expressed as \(\displaystyle \int_{a}^b f(x)\ dx = F(x) \Big|_a^b\) Where the vertical bar with the intervals of integration \(\color{black}a\) and \(\color{black}b\) as subscript and superscripts is called the evaluation bar. The evaluation bar in this case means to evaluate \(\color{black}F(x)\) at \(\color{black}x = b\) and \(\color{black}x = a\) such that \(\color{black}F(b) - F(a)\).
The theorem again, can also be equivalently expressed as \(\displaystyle \int_{a}^b f(x)\ dx = F(x) \Big]_a^b\) which rather than the vertical bar, use the square bracket. The same idea applies nonetheless, just slightly different notation. All three mentioned expressions of the theorem are equivalent and can be used interchangeably.

Continuity

A function is said to be continuous at a point \(\color{black}{c}\) if its graph can be traced with a pencil without lifting the pencil from the page at \(\color{black}{c}\). If this is not possible, that is you must lift the pencil up then down again to trace the function, then it is discontinuous at that point. Continuity can be thought of as the property of a function that allows it to be "smooth" and connected, without sudden jumps or breaks in its graph. A continuous function is a function that has no abrupt changes in value —— known as discontinuities, throughout its domain. The class of functions that qualifies as being continuous are; but not limited to; polynomial, trigonometric, exponential, logarithmic, and power functions.
There are several concepts in Calculus that relies on the assumption that the function is continuous. Some of which includes: — The definition of the derivative: The derivative of a function at a point is defined as the limit of the difference quotient as the size of the interval approaches zero. This definition relies on the function being continuous at the point in question, because the limit only exists if the function is continuous.

— The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: This theorem states that the definite integral of a function over a given interval can be found by evaluating the function at the endpoints of the interval and taking the difference. This theorem relies on the function being continuous over the interval in question, because the definite integral is only defined for continuous functions.

— The Mean Value Theorem: This theorem states that if a function is continuous on an interval and differentiable on the interior of the interval, then there exists at least one point in the interval where the derivative of the function is equal to the slope of the secant line through the two endpoints of the interval. This theorem relies on the function being continuous and differentiable on the interval in question.

— The Intermediate Value Theorem: This theorem states that if a function is continuous on an interval and takes on two different values at the endpoints of the interval, then it must take on all values between those two endpoints at some point in the interval. This theorem relies on the function being continuous on the interval in question.

— The Extreme Value Theorem: The extreme value theorem states that every continuous function on a closed, bounded interval has a maximum and minimum value on that interval. The extreme value theorem relies on the concept of continuity, as it requires that the function be continuous over the entire interval. If the function is discontinuous at any point within the interval, the theorem may not be applicable or may not have a well-defined result.

Continuous at A Point Definition

A function \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) is [continuous at a point \(\color{black}{c}\)] if and only if the following three conditions are satisfied: i. \(\color{black}{f(c)}\) is defined ii. \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to c}f(x)}\) exists iii. \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to c}f(x) = f(c)}\) A function \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) is said to be [discontinuous at a point \(\color{black}{c}\)] if any of the three conditions are not satisfied. If \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) is discontinuous at \(\color{black}{c}\), then
  • \(\color{black}{f}\) has a removable discontinuity at \(\color{black}{c}\) if \(\color{black}{\lim_{x\to c} f(x)}\) exists; it satisfies condition ii but not necessarily the other conditions.
  • \(\color{black}{f}\) has a jump discontinuity at \(\color{black}{c}\) if \(\color{black}{\lim_{x\to c} f(x)}\) does not exist; this could be because the two one-sided limits are not equal.
  • \(\color{black}{f}\) has a infinite discontinuity at \(\color{black}{c}\) if \(\color{black}{\lim_{x\to c^+} f(x)= \pm \infty}\) or \(\color{black}{\lim_{x\to c^-}f(x) = \pm \infty}\); there is an asymptote.
'continuous at a point \(\color{black}{c}\) or discontinuous at a point \(\color{black}{c}\)' can alternatively be written as ' continuous at \(\color{black}{x = c}\) or discontinuous at \(\color{black}{x = c}\)' respectively
Discontinuities take on several appearances. Discontinuities are classified into types, including removable discontinuity, infinite discontinuity, and jump discontinuity. Discontinuities that are not a removable discontinuity are categorized as non-removable discontinuities, which includes infinite discontinuity, and jump discontinuity. One characteristic of each type of discontinuity is their graphical appearance.
  • Removable discontinuities appear to be a 'missing' point that can be 'filled in'. There might also be a single dot above or below that missing point.
  • Jump discontinuities has sections of the function 'broken apart'. At a certain point, the function suddenly "steps" from one spot to another.
  • Infinite discontinuities has asymptotes.

Determining Continuity and Identifying Type of Discontinuity

To determine whether a function is continuous at a given point \(\color{black}{x = c}\), ensure that the three conditions i, ii, and iii are all satisfied at \(\color{black}{x = c}\). The strategy is to check whether conditions i and ii are satisfied before testing for condition iii. If that is not the case, it can be concluded that the function is discontinuous at \(\color{black}{x = c}\) and is unnecessary to go any further. 1.) Check if \(\color{black}{f(c)}\) is defined or if \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to c}f(x)}\) exists; if \(\color{black}{f(c)}\) is undefined or if \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to c}f(x)}\) does not exist, we can conclude the function is discontinuous at that point and go no further.
2.) If and only if \(\color{black}{f(c)}\) is defined and \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to c}f(x)}\) exists, confirm the equality between \(\color{black}{f(c)}\) and \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to c}f(x)}\). If \(\color{black}{f(c)} \neq \) \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to c}f(x)}\), then the function is discontinuous at \(\color{black}{x = c}\) Below are examples providing detailed steps on determining continuity of a function \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) at \(\color{black}{x = c}\) and identifying the type of discontinuity. The type of discontinuity can be identified by determining the limit of \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) as \(\color{black}x\) approaches \(\color{black}c\).

Continuity of a Function Over An Interval

Recap on interval notations and the associated terminology:
  • An open interval does not include its endpoints and is denoted with parentheses or with outward-facing square brackets (this notation is more common in European countries); e.g. \(\color{black}(0, 10)\) or \(\color{black}]0, 10[\)
  • A closed interval includes all its endpoints, and is denoted with square brackets; e.g. \(\color{black}[0, 10]\)
  • A half-open, half-closed, semi-open, or semi-closed interval includes only one of its endpoints, and is denoted by mixing the notations for open and closed intervals; e.g. \(\color{black}(0, 10]\) or \(\color{black}[0, 10)\)
  • The interior of an interval \(\color{black}I\) is the largest open interval that is contained in \(\color{black}I\); it is also the set of points in \(\color{black}I\) which are not endpoints of \(\color{black}I\).

A function is continuous on an open interval if the interval is contained in the domain of the function, and the function is continuous at every point of the interval. A function that is continuous on the interval \(\displaystyle \color{black}{(-\infty ,+\infty )}\) (the whole real line) is often called simply a continuous function; one says also that such a function is continuous everywhere or continuous over its entire domain. For example, all polynomial functions are continuous everywhere. A function is continuous on a semi-open or a closed interval, if the interval is contained in the domain of the function, and the function is continuous at every interior point of the interval, and the value of the function at each endpoint is the limit of the values of the function when the variable tends to the endpoint from the interior of the interval. That is, the last condition for a continuous on a closed interval as mentioned is that if we were given some function \(\color{black}f(x)\) that is continuous on a closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\), then it follows that \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a^{^+}}f(x) = f(a) \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad \text{(1) } f(x) \text{ is continuous from the right of } a\) \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to b^{^-}}f(x) = f(b) \,\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad \text{(2) } f(x) \text{ is continuous from the left of } b\) or in the case of a semi-open interval: if we were given some function \(\color{black}f(x)\) that is continuous on a semi-open interval\(\color{black}(a, b]\), then it follows that \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to b^{^-}}f(x) = f(b) \,\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad \text{(1) } f(x) \text{ is continuous from the left of } b\) because \(\color{black}b\) is the only endpoint

Relation to differentiability and integrability

The absolute value function is continuous, but fails to be differentiable at \(x = 0\) since the tangent slopes do not approach the same value from the left as they do from the right.
Every differentiable function must also be continuous; if \(\color{black}f\) is differentiable at \(\color{black}x = c\), then \(\color{black}f\) must also be continuous at \(\color{black}x = c\) However, the converse of these two propositions does not hold; continuity does not imply differentiability. Consider the absolute value function \(\color{black}f(x) = |x|\) as an example. \(\color{black}f(x)\) is continuous but is not differentiable; although \(\color{black}f(x)\) is continuous at every point in its domain, its not differentiable at \(\color{black}x = 0\) since the tangent slopes do not approach the same value from the left as they do from the right. \(\color{black}|x|\) has what is known as a cusp/corner. Continuous functions can have cusps, but not differentiable functions. In short, all differentiable functions are continuous but not all continuous functions are differentiable
Every continuous function defined on a compact interval \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\) \(f : [a,\ b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is integrable over \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\) —— e.g., Riemann-integrable, Lebesgue-integrable. Note that this proposition only applies if \(\color{black}f\) is continuous over a closed and bounded interval \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\).

Intermediate Value Theorem

Intermediate value theorem: Let \(\color{black}f\) be a continuous function defined on \(\color{black}[a,b]\) and let \(\color{black}u\) be a number with \(\color{black}f(a) < u < f(b)\). Then there exists some \(\color{black}c\) between \(\color{black}a\) and \(\color{black}b\) such that \(\color{black}f(c)=u\).
The intermediate value theorem (abbr. IVT) is an existence theorem, asserting that a continuous function will take on any value in a bounded interval:
(Intermediate Value Theorem) If a real-valued function \(\color{black}f\) is continuous on the closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\), and \(\color{black}u\) is a number between \(\color{black}f(a)\) and \(\color{black}f(b)\), that is, \(\color{black}\min(f(a), f(b)) < u < \max(f(a), f(b)),\) there exists \(\color{black}c \in [a,b]\) such that \(\color{black}f(c) = u\)
The intermediate value theorem does not assert at which point the function will take on a given value; it only guarantees the existence of such a value. The theorem asserts that if a function is continuous on a closed interval, it must take on all values between its endpoints at some point in the interval. This seems intuitively reasonable since a continuous function can be thought of as a smooth curve, and that the curve must pass through every intermediate point between the two endpoints. The intermediate value theorem captures an intuitive property of continuous functions over the real numbers: given that \(\color{black}f\) is continuous over the interval \(\color{black}[1,3]\) with the known values \(\color{black}f(1)=3\) and \(\color{black}f(2)=6\), then the graph of \(\color{black}y=f(x)\) must pass through the horizontal line \(\color{black}y=4\) and \(\color{black}y=5\) while \(\color{black}x\) moves from 1 to 3 according to the theorem. It represents the idea that the graph of a continuous function on a closed interval can be drawn without lifting a pencil from the paper.

Limits

Animated visual representation of a limit. As \(\color{black}x\) approaches but never reaching \(\color{black}a\) at any finite point from the left and right of \(\color{black}a\), the function value \(\color{black}f\) approaches \(\color{black}g\) —— the limit
In general, a limit is the value that a function approaches as the variable approaches some value, but never reaches it at any finite point. Limits are a fundamental concept in Calculus and mathematical analysis, used to determine the behavior of a function as the input variable approaches a specific value. they appear in the formal definition of derivatives, integrals, and Taylor series expansions. Limits are also used to study the behavior of functions near certain points, such as asymptotes, minima, maxima, and turning points, providing a clear understanding of the function in a neighborhood of the point. They are commonly alongside continuity, taught in an introductory-level Calculus course. Limits are used in other branches of mathematics such as Real Analysis, Complex Analysis, Functional Analysis, and Differential Equations. Consider the function: \(\displaystyle \color{black}{f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}}\) At \(\color{black}x = 1\), \(\color{black}f(x)\) evaluates to an undefined expression. \(\displaystyle \color{black}{f(1) = \frac{1^2 - 1}{1 - 1}} = \frac{0}{0}\) Such an expression gives no information about what is going on with the function nearby. We cannot determine the behavior of \(\color{black}f\) at \(\color{black}x = 1\) by ordinary methods. According to the graph of \(\color{black}f(x)\), there is a removable discontinuity —— a hole to be specific, at \(\color{black}x = 1\). To determine the function's behavior in this case, we can use the concept of limits. \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to 1}f(x) = 2}\) Although \(\color{black}f(x)\) is undefined at \(\color{black}x = 1\), applying the concept of limits lets us determine that it approaches 2 as \(\color{black}x\) approaches 1.

Informal Definition of a Limit of a Function

In the informal definition of a limit, a function is said to have a limit \(\color{black}L\) at \(\color{black}c\) if it is possible to make the function arbitrary close to \(\color{black}L\) by choosing values of \(\color{black}x\) sufficiently close to \(\color{black}c\) but never reaching it at any finite point. Finding a limit entails understanding some real-valued function \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) behaves near a point at \(\color{black}c \in \mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty, \infty\}\). It will be useful to establish some notation. The expression 'the limit of \(\color{black}f\) as \(\color{black}x\) approaches \(\color{black}c\)' describes a number, often referred to as \(\color{black}L\) in mathematical writing, that the value \(\color{black}f\) nears as \(\color{black}x\) —— the independent variable, nears \(\color{black}c\). The general notation for the limit of a function can be written as \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x \to c}}\ f(x) = L\) Which is read as "the limit of function \(\color{black}f\) as \(\color{black}x\) approaches \(\color{black}c\) equals \(\color{black}L\)". Or alternatively written as \(\displaystyle \color{black}{f(x) \rightarrow L \text{ as } x \rightarrow c}\) Which is read as "\(\color{black}{f(x)}\) tends to \(\color{black}L\) as \(\color{black}x\) tends to \(\color{black}c\)". There is a formal definition for limits called the Epsilon-Delta Definition or \(\color{black}(\epsilon,\ \delta)\) or \(\color{black}\epsilon \text{-}\)\(\color{black}\delta\), which is an algebraically precise formulation of evaluating the limit of a function. The informal definition is consistent with methods used to evaluate limits in elementary calculus, but the mathematically rigorous language associated with it appears in higher-level analysis.   • \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) oscillates between two fixed values as \(\color{black}{x}\) approaches \(\color{black}c\)

There is a case that arises when evaluating a limit, which are indeterminate forms. Indeterminate forms are undefined expressions that results from evaluating the limit via algebraic substitution. These sort of values does not reflect the actual limit of the function. Note that the adjective indeterminate does not imply that the limit does not exist. In many cases, algebraic elimination, L'HÃ´pital's rule, or other methods can be used to manipulate the expression so that the limit can be evaluated.
There are 7 indeterminate forms which are typically considered in mathematical literature: \(\displaystyle \frac{0}{0},\ \frac{\infty}{\infty},\ 0 \times \infty,\ \infty - \infty,\ 0^0,\ 1^{\infty},\ {\infty}^0\) For example, consider the function \(\color{black}{f(x) = \Large \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}}\) and its limit as \(\color{black}x\) approaches 1: \(\displaystyle \color{black}{\lim_{x\to 1}f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}}\) If we were to evaluate this limit via algebraic substitution with the argument 1, \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) would evaluate to one of the seven indeterminate forms: \(\large \color{black}{\frac{0}{0}}\). This value does not reflect the actual limit of \(\color{black}{f(x)}\). Rather than algebraic substitution, other methods can be used to determine the actual limit. Not every undefined algebraic expression corresponds to an indeterminate form. For example, the expression \(\large \color{black}\frac{1}{0}\) is undefined as a real number but does not correspond to an indeterminate form; any defined limit that gives rise to this form will diverge to infinity.

Evaluating Limits of Functions

Evaluating the limit of a function \(\color{black}f(x)\) means to find the limiting value \(\color{black}L\) as \(\color{black}x\) approaches \(\color{black}c\). Two possible values of \(\color{black}c\) are \(\color{black}c \in \mathbb{R}\) or is boundless: \(\color{black}\pm \infty\). There is no single uniform method of evaluating a limit. There are various methods of evaluating the limit of a function, and some are more advantageous than the other. The limit laws or properties are proven mathematical theorems that simplifies the process of evaluating a limit. For example, its proven that every polynomial function \(\color{black}p(x)\) is continuous over its entire domain, hence evaluating its limit is simple as directly substituting the value \(\color{black}c\) with the independent variable and solving. That is, \(\color{black}\lim_{x\to c}p(x) = p(c)\). Below are the various methods used to evaluate a limit.

Limit Laws and Properties

Let \(\{n,\ k\} \in \mathbb{R}\), and \(f\) and \(g\) be functions, and where \(\displaystyle A = \lim_{x\to c}f(x),\ B = \lim_{x\to c}g(x)\).

Constant, k

Let \(k \in \mathbb{R}\). The limit of [a constant] is the constant. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c} k = k\)

Constant k times a function

Let \(k \in \mathbb{R}\). The limit of [the product of a constant \(k\) and a function \(f\)] is equal to \(k \times A\) \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}[k \cdot f(x)] = kA\)

Sum of Functions

The limit of [the sum of two functions \(f\) and \(g\)] is equal to the sum of their limits: \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\, [f(x) + g(x)] = A + B\) Which can be generalized to an arbitrary n amount of functions involved. The limit of the addition function operation containing n operands is equal to the combined sum of each limit of each function involved. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\, [f_1(x) +\ ... \ + f_n(x)] = \lim_{x\to c} f_1(x) +\ ... \ + \lim_{x\to c} f_n(x)\)

Difference of Functions

The limit of [the difference of two functions \(f\) and \(g\)] is equal to the difference of their limits: \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\, [f(x) - g(x)] = A - B\) Which can be generalized to an arbitrary n amount of functions involved. The limit of the subtraction function operation containing n operands is equal to the combined difference of each limit of each function involved. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\, [f_1(x) -\ ... \ - f_n(x)] = \lim_{x\to c} f_1(x) -\ ... \ - \lim_{x\to c} f_n(x)\)

Product of Functions

The limit of [the product of two functions \(f\) and \(g\)] is equal to the product of their limits: \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\, [f(x)g(x)] = A \times B\) Which can be generalized to an arbitrary n amount of functions involved. The limit of the multiplication function operation containing n operands is equal to the combined product of each limit of each function involved. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\, [f_1(x) \times \ ... \ \times f_n(x)] = \lim_{x\to c} f_1(x) \times \ ... \ \times \lim_{x\to c} f_n(x)\)

Quotient of Functions

The limit of [the quotient of two functions \(f\) and \(g\)] is equal to the quotient of their limits, assuming that the limit of \(g\) is not 0: \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\, \left[ \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \right] = \frac{A}{B}\ \Big|\ B \neq 0\)

Function raised to an exponent

Let \(n \in \mathbb{R}\). The limit of [a function \(f\) raised to an exponent \(n\)] is equal to the limit of \(f\) raised to the power of \(n\) \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}\, [f(x)]^n= A^n\)

Properties of Limits involving Infinity

One-Sided Limits

As \(\color{black}{x}\) approaches from the left of \(\color{black}{x = 0}\), \(\color{black}f\) approaches 1. As \(\color{black}{x}\) approaches from the right of \(\color{black}{x = 0}\), \(\color{black}f\) approaches 2.
In this case, \(\color{black}{\lim_{\large x\to c^-} \normalsize f(x) = 1}\) and \(\color{black}{\lim_{\large x\to c^+} \normalsize f(x) = 2}\). Incidentally, the two-sided limit does not exist because \(\color{black}{\lim_{\large x\to c^-} \normalsize f(x) \neq \lim_{\large x\to c^+} \normalsize f(x)}\)
A one-sided limit refers to either one of the two limits of a function \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) of a real variable \(\color{black}x \) as \( \color{black}x \) approaches a specified point either from the left or from the right. Practically speaking, when evaluating a left-hand limit, we consider only values of \(x\) "to the left of \(c\)," i.e., where \(\color{black}{x < c}\) . A similar statement holds for evaluating right-hand limits; there we consider only values of \( \color{black}x \) to the right of \(\color{black}c\), i.e., where \(\color{black}{x>c}\). It is possible for exactly one of the two one-sided limits to exist (while the other does not exist). It is also possible for neither of the two one-sided limits to exist. The limit as \(\color{black}x\) approaches \(\color{black}a\) is sometimes called a "two-sided limit". When the one-sided limits are unequal or does not exist, then the limit does not exist (or also the two-sided limit does not exist). In other words, we say \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) has a limit \(\color{black}L\) as \(\color{black}x\) approaches \(\color{black}c\), if both its right and left hand limits are equal. That is if, \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c^-}f(x) = \lim_{x\to c^+}f(x) = L\) then \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c}f(x) = L\) otherwise if \(\displaystyle \lim_{x\to c^-}f(x) \neq \lim_{x\to c^+}f(x) \iff \lim_{x\to c}f(x) = \text{DNE}\)

Differential Calculus

In mathematics, differential calculus is a subfield of calculus that studies the rates at which quantities change —— the derivative. It is one of the two traditional divisions of calculus, the other being integral calculus —— the study of the area beneath a curve. The primary objects of study in differential calculus are the derivative of a function, related notions such as the differential, and their applications. The derivative of a function at a chosen input value describes the rate of change of the function near that input value. The process of finding a derivative is called differentiation. Geometrically, the derivative at a point is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of the function at that point, provided that the derivative exists and is defined at that point. For a real-valued function of a single real variable, the derivative of a function at a point generally determines the best linear approximation to the function at that point. Differential calculus and integral calculus are connected by the fundamental theorem of calculus, which states that differentiation is the reverse process to integration.

Notation for differentiation

There is no single uniform notation for differentiation. Instead, various notations for the derivative of a function or variable have been proposed by various mathematicians. The usefulness of each notation varies with the context, and it is sometimes advantageous to use more than one notation in a given context. The more commonly used notations are Leibniz's and Lagrange's.

Leibniz's Notation for Differentiation

Leibniz's notation, named in honor of the 17th-century German philosopher and mathematician Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, uses the symbols \(\color{black}dy\) and \(\color{black}dx\) to represent infinitesimal increments of \(\color{black}y\) and \(\color{black}x\), respectively, just as \(\color{black}\Delta y\) and \(\color{black}\Delta x\) represent finite increments of \(\color{black}x\) and \(\color{black}y\), respectively. Suppose a dependent variable \(\color{black}y\) represents a function \(\color{black}f\) of an independent variable \(\color{black}x\), that is, \(y = f(x)\) Then the derivative of the function \(\color{black}f\) with respect to \(\color{black}x\), in Leibniz's notation for differentiation, can be written as \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}\ \ \text{or}\ \ \frac{d}{dx}y\) The notation may also be thought of as an operator, operating on a function, which in the case above, is \(\color{black}y\). In its modern interpretation, the expression \(\large \color{black}\frac{dy}{dx}\) should not be read as the division of two infinitesimal quantities dx and dy (as Leibniz had envisioned it); rather, the whole expression should be seen as a single symbol that is shorthand for \(\displaystyle \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\) (note \(\color{black} \Delta\) vs. \(\color{black}d\), where \(\color{black} \Delta\) indicates a infinite difference) For an introductory-level Calculus course, it is sufficient to view this notation as no more than an operator, a syntatical device used to indicate the function of differentiation with respect to a variable. Furthermore the derivative of \(\color{black}f(x)\) can be written as: \(\displaystyle \frac{df}{dx}(x)\ \ \text{or}\ \ \frac{df(x)}{dx} \ \ \text{or}\ \ \frac{d}{dx}f(x)\) When this notation forms an equality, i.e.: \(\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)\) The equation can be read as "the derivative of \(\color{black}y\) with respect to \(\color{black}x\) is equal to \(\color{black}f(x)\). The expression on the right-hand side is the derivative of the function that is being differentiated, which is \(\color{black}y\) in the case above. For example, if \(\color{black}y = 2x + 1\), then \(\large\color{black}\normalsize\frac{dy}{dx} = 2\). Leibniz's notation for differentiation is analogous to the corresponding Lagrange's notations: \(\displaystyle f'(x)\ \ \text{or}\ \ y'\) The derivative of \(\color{black}f(x)\) at a point \(\color{black}x = c\) can be expressed in Leibniz's notation as \( \displaystyle \left. \frac{dy}{dx} \,\middle|\right. _{x \hspace{0.11em} =\, c} \quad \text{ or } \quad \frac{dy}{dx}(c)\) Leibniz's notation allows one to specify the variable for differentiation (in the denominator). The derivative of a function \(\color{black}f(t)\) where \(\color{black}t\) is the dependent variable of \(\color{black}f\), can be expressed in Leibniz notation as: \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dt}f(t)\) Which means to differentiate \(\color{black}f(t)\) in respect to \(\color{black}t\), or if \(\color{black}a = f(s)\) is a functional relationship between dependent and independent variables \(\color{black}a\) and \(\color{black}s\), then the derivative of \(\color{black}a\) with respect to \(\color{black}s\) can be expressed in Leibniz's notation as \(\displaystyle \frac{da}{ds}\) Which means to differentiate \(\color{black}a\) with respect to \(\color{black}s\) Lebiniz's Notation for Higher-order Derivatives Given \(y = f(x)\), Lebiniz's notation for higher-order derivatives are written as \(\displaystyle \frac{d^ny}{dx^n}\) where \(\color{black}n\) is the nth derivative of \(\color{black}f(x)\). This is a suggestive notational device that comes from formal algebraic manipulations of symbols, as in, \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\left( \frac{d}{dx}f(x) \right) \rightarrow \left(\frac{d}{dx}\right)^2 f(x) \rightarrow \frac{d^2}{d^2x} f(x)\) The higher derivatives can be obtained inductively based on the expression above. So the second, third, fourth, and nth derivatives of \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) can be written as respectively: \(\displaystyle \frac{d^2y}{dx^2},\frac{d^3y}{dx^3},\frac{d^4y}{dx^4},\ ...\ ,\frac{d^ny}{dx^n}\) Leibniz's higher-order derivatives notation are analogous to the Lagrange's higher-order derivatives notation: \(\displaystyle f^{(n)}(x)\) that is, \(\displaystyle \frac{d^ny}{dx^n} = f^{(n)}(x)\) where \(\color{black}n\) is the nth derivative of function \(\color{black}f(x)\).

Lagrange's Notation for Differentiation

In Lagrange's notation, an apostrophe-like mark called a prime denotes the derivative. Thus the derivative of a function called \(\color{black}f\) or \(\color{black}f(x)\) is written \(f' \text{ or } f'(x)\) When this notation forms an equality between some function \(\color{black}f(x)\), i.e.: \(\displaystyle f'(x) = f(x)\) The right-hand side is the derivative of the function that is being differentiated. For example, if \(\color{black}f(x) = 2x + 1\), then \(\color{black}f'(x) = 2\). This notation is analogous to the Leibniz Notations: \(\displaystyle \frac{df}{dx}(x)\ \ \text{or}\ \ \frac{df(x)}{dx} \ \ \text{or}\ \ \frac{d}{dx}f(x)\) Lagrange's Notation for Higher-order Derivatives Higher derivatives are indicated using additional prime marks, as in \(\color{black}f''(x)\) for the second derivative and \(\color{black}f'''(x)\) for the third derivative. The use of repeated prime marks eventually becomes unwieldy. To continue denoting higher derivatives beyond that point, some authors use Roman numerals in superscripts, usually in lower case: \(f^{\text{iv}}(x),\ f^{\text{v}}(x),\ f^{\text{vi}}(x),\ ... \) which is the fourth, fifth, and sixth derivative. Other use Arabic numerals enclosed in parentheses as the superscript: \(f^{\text{(4)}}(x),\ f^{\text{(5)}}(x),\ f^{\text{(6)}}(x),\ ... \) This notation can be generalized to denote the \(\color{black}n^\text{th}\) derivative of \(\color{black}f\): \(f^{(n)}(x)\)

Tangent lines; linear approximation
The derivative of a function of a single variable at a chosen input value, when it exists, is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of the function at that point. The tangent line is the best linear approximation of the function near that input value. For this reason, the derivative is often described as the "instantaneous rate of change", the ratio of the instantaneous change in the dependent variable to that of the independent variable. In general terms, the derivative of a function is a measure of how rapidly a function's output changes relative to a change in the input value. Definition of a Derivative

Here is a graph of a function. Initially, there exists two points \(\color{black}(x,\ f(x))\) and \(\color{black}(x + h,\ f(x + h))\) that are \(\color{black}h\) distances away, intersected by a secant line. As \(\color{black}h\) tends to 0, point \(\color{black}(x + h,\ f(x + h))\) closes in on point \(\color{black}(x,\ f(x))\), essentially forming a tangent line.
Recall the concept of slopes. The slope is a numerical real-numbered value that describes the direction and steepness of a line in a linear equation \(\color{black}y = mx + b\). Slope may also be referred to as the average rate of change. The slope is calculated by finding the ratio of the change in \(\color{black}y\) and change in \(\color{black}x\) between two points.
The slope formula is expressed as: \(\displaystyle \color{black}m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\) Where \(\color{black}m\) is the slope, \(\color{black}(x_1,\ y_1)\) are the coordinates of the first point, and \(\color{black}(x_2,\ y_2)\) are the coordinates of the second point. The numerator represents the euclidean distance of their vertical components and the denominator as the euclidean distance of their horizontal components. Also recall a few terms:
  • A secant is a line that intersects a curve at a minimum of two distinct points. The secant line connects two points \(\color{black}(x,f(x))\) and \(\color{black}(a,f(a))\) in the Cartesian plane on a curve described by a function \(\color{black}f(x)\)
  • A tangent to a curve at a given point is the straight line that "just touches" the curve at that point.
Geometrical definition of a secant and tangent The slope formula can be generalized to an expression known as the difference quotient, whose result is a secant, measuring the average rate of change of a function \(\color{black}f(x)\) over an interval \(\color{black}[x,\ x+h]\) of length \(\color{black}h\). \(\displaystyle \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}\) Choose two values \(\color{black}\{x,\ h\ |\ x \in \mathbb{R},\ h \in \mathbb{R}\}\). The two points involved in this expression \(\color{black}P_1 = (x + h,\ f(x + h))\) and \(\color{black}P_2 = (x,\ f(x))\), are analogous to \(\color{black}y_2\) and \(\color{black}y_1\) in the slope formula respectively. \(\color{black}h\) is the change in \(\color{black}x\) between \(\color{black}P_1\) and \(\color{black}P_2\). The difference of the function values \(\color{black}f(x + h)\) and \(\color{black}f(x)\) is divided by \(\color{black}h\); the result of this difference-quotient is the slope of the secant line that would intersect points \(\color{black}P_1\) and \(\color{black}P_2\).
If \(\color{black}h\) were to approach 0 but never reach it any finite point, the secant line would tend to a tangent; in other words, if points \(\color{black}P_1\) and \(\color{black}P_2\) were brought closer together, such that \(\color{black}h\) approaches 0, the secant line essentially becomes a tangent line. The slope of the forementioned tangent line is referred to as the derivative at that point, also referred to as the instantaneous rate of change of \(\color{black}f\) at \(\color{black}x\). This slope is "instantaneous" because it is the rate of change at a single moment in time, or at that particular instant, or at that very exact moment in time, rather than over a longer interval. This differs from a secant line because the secant line is only an approximation to the behavior of the function at the point a and does not give an exact accounting for what happens between a and a + h. Now that we've established the rationale, the derivative of a real-valued function \(\color{black}f : U \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) as a function \(\color{black}f'\) is defined as \(\displaystyle f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h},\) which is the limit of the difference quotient as \(\color{black}h\) approaches 0. \(\color{black}\Delta x\) might be used instead of \(\color{black}h\) in some mathematical literature to denote the change in \(\color{black}x\). The domain of \(\color{black}f'\) is the set of all points \(\color{black}c \in U\) for which the limit[the difference quotient as \(\color{black}h\) approaches 0] exists. \(\color{black}f'\) gives the derivative (the slope of the tangnet line) of \(\color{black}f\) at each point in the domain of \(\color{black}f\) for which the derivative is defined. If we define a function \(\color{black}f(x)\) for example, then the value \(\color{black}f'(2)\) represents the slope of the tangnet line at the point \(\color{black}(2, f(2))\) of the graph of \(\color{black}f\). Differentiating a function using this definition is known as differentiation from first principles, which is what differentiation rules and identities are derived from. Using differentiation from first principles to find the derivative of \(\color{black}f(x) = x^2\) to show that \(\color{black}f'(x) = 2x\) would go as follows: \( \begin{aligned} f'(x) & =\lim _{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \\[5px] & =\lim _{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{(x + h)^2 - x^2}{h} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{h^2 + 2hx + x^2 - x^2}{h} \\[5px] & =\lim _{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{2hx + h^2}{h} \\[5px] & =\lim _{h \rightarrow 0} 2x + h \\[5px] & = 2x \end{aligned} \) This proof can be generalized to show that \(\color{black} \Large \frac{d}{dx} \normalsize [ax^n] = anx^{n - 1}\) if \(\color{black}a\) and \(\color{black}n\) are constants. This is a differentiation rule known as the power rule; for instance, using the power rule: \(\color{black} \Large \frac{d}{dx} \normalsize \left[5x^{4}\right]=5(4)x^{3}=20x^{3}\). The derivative definition can be slightly modified to determine the derivative of \(\color{black}f(x)\) at a point \(\color{black}x = c\) by substituting the independent variable \(\color{black}x\) in the definition with the value \(\color{black}c\): \(\displaystyle f'(c) = L = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h}\) this is equal to the slope of the tangent at \(\color{black}x = c\). For example, determining the derivative of \(\color{black}f(x) = x^2\) at \(\color{black}x = 5\) using differentiation from first principles would begin with the expression: \(\displaystyle f'(5) = L = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(5 + h) - f(5)}{h}\) then evaluating the limit.

Higher-order derivative

The process of differentiation can be applied several times in succession. Let \(\color{black}f\) be a differentiable function, and let \(\color{black}f'\) be its derivative. The derivative of \(\color{black}f'\) (if it has one) is written \(\color{black}f''\) and is called the second derivative of \(\color{black}f\). Similarly, the derivative of the second derivative, if it exists, is written \(\color{black}f'''\) and is called the third derivative of \(\color{black}f\). Continuing this process, one can define, if it exists, the nth derivative as the derivative of the (n - 1)th derivative. These repeated derivatives are called higher-order derivatives. The nth derivative is also called the derivative of order n

Differentiability

Recall that the derivative is defined formally as a limit, and there are cases when a limit does not exist. The derivative is a special case of a limit; there are cases where the derivative does not exist at a point. This gives rise to the concept of differentiability. If \(\color{black}x_0\) is an interior point in the domain of a function \(\color{black}f\), then \(\color{black}f\) is said to be differentiable at \(\color{black}x_0\) if the derivative \(\color{black}f'(x_0)\) exists. In other words, the graph of \(\color{black}f\) has a non-vertical tangent line at the point \(\color{black}(x_0, f(x_0))\). \(\color{black}f\) is said to be a differentiable function if the derivative exists at every interior point of its domain. \(\color{black}f\) is said to be differentiable over an open interval \(\color{black}I\) if the derivative exists at every interior point of \(\color{black}I\). That is, a real-valued function \(\color{black}f\) defined on an open interval \(\color{black}(a, b)\) is said to be differentiable at \(\color{black}x_0 \in (a, b)\) if the limit \(\displaystyle \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h}\) exists

Examples

The function \(\color{black}f(x) = \sqrt[3]{x}\) has a vertical tangent at \(\color{black}x = 0\), which can be proven as follows: \(\displaystyle \begin{align}f'(0) = L & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt[\leftroot{-1}\uproot{1}\scriptstyle 3]{0 + h} - \sqrt[\leftroot{-1}\uproot{1}\scriptstyle 3]{0}}{h} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\sqrt[\leftroot{-1}\uproot{1}\scriptstyle 3]{h} - \sqrt[\leftroot{-1}\uproot{1}\scriptstyle 3]{0} }{h} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\frac{d}{dh}\left[\sqrt[\leftroot{-1}\uproot{1}\scriptstyle 3]{h} - \sqrt[\leftroot{-1}\uproot{1}\scriptstyle 3]{0}\right] }{\frac{d}{dh}\left[h\right]} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\large \frac{1}{3h^{\frac{2}{3}}} \normalsize }{1} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{1}{3h^{\large\frac{2}{3}} \normalsize} \end{align}\) Then determining the one-sided limits: \(\displaystyle \lim_{h \to 0^{^-}}\frac{1}{3h^{\large\frac{2}{3}} \normalsize} = -\infty \quad\quad \lim_{h \to 0^{^+}}\frac{1}{3h^{\large\frac{2}{3}} \normalsize} = \infty \) The one-sided limits are unbounded, meaning the slope is infinite and there is a vertical tangent at \(\color{black}x = 0\). In fact, the graph \(\color{black}f'(x) = \displaystyle \frac{1}{3x^{\large \frac{2}{3}} \normalsize}\) has a vertical asymptote at \(\color{black}x = 0\) The function \(\color{black}f(x) = |x|\) has a nonexistent tangent at \(\color{black}x = 0\), which can be proven as follows: \(\displaystyle \begin{align}f'(0) = L & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|0 + h| - |0|}{h} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|h| - |0|}{h} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\frac{d}{dh}\left[|h| - |0|\right] }{\frac{d}{dh}\left[h\right]} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\large \frac{h}{|h|} \normalsize }{1} \\[5px] & = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{h}{|h|} \end{align}\) Then determining the one-sided limits: \(\displaystyle \lim_{h \to 0^{^-}}\frac{h}{|h|} \normalsize = -1 \quad\quad \lim_{h \to 0^{^+}}\frac{h}{|h|} \normalsize = 1 \) The one-sided limits are unequal, meaning the derivative is not defined at \(\color{black}x = 0\).

Relationship between continuity and differentiability

The absolute value function is continuous, but fails to be differentiable at \(x = 0\) since the tangent slopes do not approach the same value from the left as they do from the right.
Every differentiable function must also be continuous; if \(\color{black}f\) is differentiable at \(\color{black}x = c\), then \(\color{black}f\) must also be continuous at \(\color{black}x = c\) However, the converse of these two propositions does not hold; continuity does not imply differentiability. Consider the absolute value function \(\color{black}f(x) = |x|\) as an example. \(\color{black}f(x)\) is continuous but is not differentiable; although \(\color{black}f(x)\) is continuous at every point in its domain, its not differentiable at \(\color{black}x = 0\) since the tangent slopes do not approach the same value from the left as they do from the right. \(\color{black}|x|\) has what is known as a cusp/corner. Continuous functions can have cusps, but not differentiable functions.

Differentiation Rules

Rather than differentiating functions from the first principle, differentiation rules can be used instead. Differentiation rules are formulae that allow us to find the derivatives of functions quickly.

Elementary rules of Differentiation

The elementary rules of differentiation are the most basic and fundamental rules of differentiation in Calculus. The elementary rules of differentiation are fundamental because they apply to a wide variety of functions, including some of the most commonly used functions in calculus. These rules provide a starting point for finding the derivatives of more complicated functions, and they can be combined in various ways to find the derivatives of more complex expressions.

Constant Rule

For any value of \(\color{black}c\), where \(\color{black} c \in \mathbb{R}\), \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dy}c = 0,\) or if \(\color{black}f(x)\) is the constant function given by \(\color{black}f(x)=c\), then \(\color{black}f'(x) = 0\).

Constant Multiple Rule

If \(\color{black}f(x)\) is differentiable and \(\color{black}c\) is any constant, then \([cf(x)]' = cf'(x)\)

Sum and Difference Rule

If \(\color{black}f(x)\) and \(\color{black}g(x)\) are differentiable functions, then \([f(x) - g(x)]' = f'(x) - g'(x) \quad\quad\quad [f(x) + g(x)]' = f'(x) + g'(x)\)

Product Rule

If \(\color{black}f(x)\) and \(\color{black}g(x)\) are differentiable functions, then \([f(x) \cdot g(x)]' = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)\)

Quotient Rule

Let \(\color{black}f(x)\) and \(\color{black}g(x)\) be a function, \(\displaystyle \left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right]' = \frac{f'(x)g(x) - g'(x)f(x)}{g^2(x)}\)

Power Rule

Let \(\color{black}f\) be a function satisfying \(\color{black}f(x)=x^{n}\), where \(\color{black}n \in \mathbb{R} \). Then, \(\displaystyle f'(x) = nx^{n-1}\) When \(\color{black}f\) is specifically in the form of \(\color{black}f(x) = x\), then \(\color{black}f'(x) = 1\), because \(\color{black}x\) is being raised to the power of 1, and \(\color{black}1 - 1 = 0\); anything powered to 0 is just 1.

Chain Rule

Compositions are common in typical looking functions. For example, the function \(\color{black}f(x) = \sqrt{x^2 + 9}\) is composition of the radical function \(\color{black}\sqrt{x}\) with a polynomial function \(\color{black}x^2 + 9\); or that \(\color{black}f(x) = (2x + 1)^2\) is composition of the power function \(\color{black}x^2\) with a polynomial function \(\color{black}2x + 1\). The chain rule is a formula that expresses the derivative of the composition of two differentiable functions \(\color{black}f\) and \(\color{black}g\). If \(\color{black}h = f \circ g\) is the function such that \(\color{black}h(x) = f(g(x))\), then the chain rule, in Lagranage's notation, states that \(\color{black}h'(x) = f'(g(x))g'(x)\) or equivalently, in terms of function composition \(\displaystyle \color{black}(f \circ g)' = (f' \circ g) \cdot g'\) The chain rule may also be expressed in Leibniz's notation. If a variable \(\color{black}z\) depends on the variable \(\color{black}y\), which itself depends on the variable \(\color{black}x\) (that is, \(\color{black}y\) and \(\color{black}z\) are dependent variables), then \(\color{black}z\) depends on \(\color{black}x\) as well, via the intermediate variable y; this relationship can be expressed in function notation as \(\color{black} z = f(g(x))\) and \(\color{black}y = g(x)\). In this case, the chain rule is expressed as \(\displaystyle \frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{dz}{dy} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}\) So the chain rule states that the derivative of a composition is [the derivative of the outer function(leaving the inner function unchanged and in place)] times [the derivative of the inner function]. placeholder

The Extreme Value Theorem (abbr. EVT) guarantees the existence of the extrema of a continuous function defined on a closed and bounded interval. Recap on the relevant mathematical terminologies:

    (Extreme Value Theorem) If a real-valued function \(\color{black}f\) is continuous on the closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\), then \(\color{black}f\) must attain a maximum and a minimum, each at least once. That is, there exist numbers \(\color{black}m\) and \(\color{black}M\) in \(\color{black}[a, b]\) such that: \(f(m) \leq f(x) \leq f(M) \quad \forall x \in [a,b]\)

If either of those conditions does not hold, then the theorem does not apply. It is also important to note the theorem states the maximum and minimum will occur at least once over the interval, not how to find them.

    Mean Value Theorem

    Rolle's Theorem
    If a real-valued function \(\color{black}f\) is continuous on a closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\), differentiable on the open interval \(\color{black}(a, b)\), and \(\color{black}f(a) = f(b)\), then there exists a \(\color{black}c\) in the open interval \(\color{black}(a, b)\) such that \(\color{black}f'(c) = 0\).
    Rolle's theorem or Rolle's lemma essentially states that any real-valued differentiable function that attains equal values at two distinct points must have at least one stationary point somewhere between them — that is, a point where the first derivative (the slope of the tangent line to the graph of the function) is zero.
    (Rolle's Theorem) If a real-valued function \(\color{black}f\) is continuous on a closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\), differentiable on the open interval \(\color{black}(a, b)\), and \(\color{black}f(a) = f(b)\), then there exists at least one \(\color{black}c\) in the open interval \(\color{black}(a, b)\) such that \(f'(c) = 0.\) That is, there exists a point where the first derivative (the slope of the tangent line to the graph of the function) is zero.
    This version of Rolle's theorem is used to prove the Mean Value Theorem, of which Rolle's theorem is a special case. The MVT is a more general result than Rolle's Theorem, as it does not require the function to have equal values at the endpoints of the interval. Mean Value Theorem
    For any function that is continuous on \(\color{black}[a,b]\) and differentiable on \(\color{black}(a,b)\) there exists some \(\color{black}c\) in the interval \(\color{black}(a,b)\) such that the secant joining the endpoints of the interval \(\color{black}[a,b]\) is parallel to the tangent at \(\color{black}c\).
    The Mean Value Theorem (abbr. MVT), also known as Lagrange's mean value theorem (LMVT), is an existence theorem that provides a formal framework for a fairly intuitive statement relating change in a function to the behavior of its derivative. The theorem states that the derivative of a continuous and differentiable function must attain the function's average rate of change (in a given interval). For a physical intuition, if a car travels 100 miles in 2 hours, then it must have had the exact speed of 50 mph at some point in time. Simple-sounding as it is, the mean value theorem actually lies at the heart of the proof of the fundamental theorem of calculus, and is itself based ultimately on properties of the real numbers. There is also a mean value theorem for definite integrals.
    (Mean Value Theorem) The theorem states that if a real-valued function \(\color{black}f\) is continuous on the closed interval \(\color{black}[a,b]\) and differentiable on the open interval \(\color{black}(a,b)\), then there exists some \(\color{black}c\) in \(\color{black}(a,b)\) such that \(\displaystyle f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a};\) the slope of the tangent at \(\color{black}c\) is parallel to the secant line intersecting through the endpoints \(\color{black}(a, f(a))\) and \(\color{black}(b, f(b)\), that is.
    The mean value theorem is a generalization of Rolle's theorem, which assumes \(\color{black}f(a)=f(b)\), so that the right-hand side above is zero. Geometrically, the left-hand side of the MVT represents the slope of the tangent line to \(\color{black}f(x)\) at \(\color{black}x=c\), and the right-hand side as the slope of the secant line connecting the points \(\color{black}(a,f(a))\) and \(\color{black}(b,f(b))\); in other words, the left-hand side is the derivative of \(\color{black}f\) at a point \(\color{black}c\), and the right-hand side is the slope of the secant line. Conceptually, the left-hand side represents the instantaneous rate of change of f(x) at x=c while the right-hand side represents the average rate of change of f(x) over the interval from x=a to x=b

    Logarithmic Differentiation

    Logarithmic differentiation or differentiation by taking logarithms is a method used to differentiate functions by employing the logarithmic derivative of a function. Following the chain rule, the logarithmic derivative of a function \(\color{black}f(x)\) is defined as \(\displaystyle [\ln(f(x))]' = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} \) Isolating \(\color{black}f'(x)\) in the right side, the general logarithmic differentiation formula is defined as \(f'(x) = f(x) \cdot [\, \ln(f(x))\,]'\) The technique is often performed in cases where it is easier to differentiate the logarithm of a function rather than the function itself. This usually occurs in cases where the function of interest is composed of a product, is a quotient of two functions, or is a function raised to a function —— so that a logarithmic transformation will turn it into separate parts (which is much easier to differentiate). The method is used because the properties of logarithms provide avenues to quickly simplify complicated functions to be differentiated. These properties are manipulated after the taking of natural logarithms on both sides and before the preliminary differentiation. Let \(\color{black}u\) and \(\color{black}v\) be functions; the logarithimic laws that will be considered in this section are \(\displaystyle \begin{align} & \ln(uv) = \ln(u) + \ln(v) && \implies (\ln uv)' = \frac{u'}{u} + \frac{v'}{v}, \\[5px] & \ln\left(\frac{u}{v}\right) = \ln(u) - \ln(v) && \implies \left(\ln \frac{u}{v}\right)' = \frac{u'}{u} - \frac{v'}{v}, \\[5px] & \ln(u^v) = v \ln (u) && \implies (\ln u^v)' = v'\ln(u) + v[\ln(u)]' \end{align}\)

    Product of Functions

    Logarithmic differentiation is used in one of many proofs for the product rule. Let the product of two functions be represented by \(\color{black}h(x)\), where \(h(x) = f(x)g(x).\) Take the natural logarithm of \(\color{black}h(x)\): \(\ln(h(x)),\) then applying properties of the logarithm \(\color{black}(\ln uv) = \ln(u) + \ln(v)\), there is the equation \(\ln(h(x)) = \ln(f(x)) + \ln(g(x)).\) Take the logarithmic derivative of both sides of the equation \(\displaystyle \frac{h'(x)}{h(x)} = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} + \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)},\) then isolate and solve for \(\color{black}h'(x)\) using algebraic manipulation on the right side of the equation: \(\displaystyle \begin{align} h'(x) & = h(x) \cdot \left\{ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} + \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} \right\} \\[5px] & = f(x)g(x) \cdot \left\{ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} + \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} \right\} \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad && (1) \text{ Expand } h(x) \\[5px] & = \frac{f'(x)f(x)g(x)}{f(x)} + \frac{f(x)g(x)g'(x)}{g(x)} && (2) \text{ Perform algebraic manipulation}\\[5px] & = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x) && (3) \text{ Perform algebraic manipulation, simplify} \end{align}\)

    Quotient of Functions

    Logarithmic differentiation is used in one of many proofs for the quotient rule. Let the quotient of two functions be represented by \(\color{black}h(x)\), where \(\displaystyle h(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) Take the natural logarithm of \(\color{black}h(x)\): \(\ln(h(x)),\) then after applying the logarithm property \(\color{black}(\ln \frac{u}{v}) = \ln(u) - \ln(v)\), there is the equation \(\ln(h(x)) = \ln(f(x)) - \ln(g(x)).\) Take the derivative of both sides of the equation via the logarithmic derivative \(\displaystyle \frac{h'(x)}{h(x)} = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} - \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)},\) then isolate and solve for \(\color{black}h'(x)\) using algebraic manipulation on the right side of the equation: \(\displaystyle \begin{align} h'(x) & = h(x) \cdot \left\{ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} - \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} \right\} \\[5px] & = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \cdot \left\{ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} - \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)} \right\} \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad && (1) \text{ Expand } h(x) \\[5px] & = \frac{f(x)f'(x)}{g(x)f(x)} - \frac{f(x)g'(x)}{g^2(x)} && (2) \text{ Perform algebraic manipulation; } \frac{A}{B} \times \frac{C}{D} = \frac{A \times C}{B \times D}\\[5px] & = \frac{f'(x)}{g(x)} - \frac{f(x)g'(x)}{g^2(x)} && (3) \text{ Cancel like terms} \\[5px] & = g(x) \left[ \frac{f'(x)}{g(x)} \right] - \frac{f(x)g'(x)}{g^2(x)} && (3) \text{ Perform algebraic manipulation; convert fractions so that they are of equivalent denominators.} \\[-20px] & && \quad \;\, \text{Identify the lowest common denominator, which is } g^2(x), \text{ so multiply the left fraction by } g(x). \\[10px] & = \frac{f'(x)g(x)}{g^2(x)} - \frac{f(x)g'(x)}{g^2(x)} && \sim(3) \\[6px] & = \frac{f'(x)g(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{g^2(x)} && (4) \frac{A}{B} - \frac{C}{B} = \frac{A - C}{B} \end{align}\)

    Functions raised to functions

    Let a function \(\color{black}f(x)\) raised to the function \(\color{black}g(x)\) be represented by \(\color{black}h(x)\), where \(\displaystyle h(x) = f(x)^{g(x)}\) To logarithmically differentiate \(\color{black}h(x)\), begin with taking the natural logarithm of \(\color{black}h(x)\): \(\ln(h(x)),\) then after applying the logarithm property \(\color{black}(\ln u^v) = v\ln(u)\), there is the equation \(\ln(h(x)) = g(x)\ln(f(x)).\) Take the derivative of the right side using the product rule, and left side using the logarithm derivative \(\displaystyle \frac{h'(x)}{h(x)} = g'(x) \ln(f(x)) + g(x) \, [\ln(f(x))]',\) then isolate and solve for \(\color{black}h'(x)\) using algebraic manipulation on the right side of the equation: \(\displaystyle \begin{align} h'(x) & = h(x) \times \left\{ g'(x) \ln(f(x)) + g(x) \, \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} \right\} \\[5px] & = f(x)^{g(x)} \times \left\{ g'(x) \ln(f(x)) + g(x) \, \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} \right\} \quad\quad\quad && (1) \text{ Expand } h(x). \text{No further algebraic manipulation needed} \\[5px] \end{align}\)

    Increasing and Decreasing Functions

    Let \(\color{black}f\) be a function defined on an interval \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\), and for any two values \(\color{black}x_1\) and \(\color{black}x_2\) in the interval,
    • \(\color{black}f\) is increasing on \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\) if for every \(\color{black}x_1 < x_2\) in \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\), \(\color{black}f(x_1) \leq f(x_2)\)
    • \(\color{black}f\) is decreasing on \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\) if for every \(\color{black}x_1 < x_2\) in \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\), \(\color{black}f(x_1) \geq f(x_2)\)

    Let \(\color{black}f\) be a continuous function defined on an interval \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\) and differentiable on \(\color{black}(a,\ b)\)
    • If \(\color{black}f'(c) > 0\) for all \(\color{black}c\) in \(\color{black}(a,\ b)\), then \(\color{black}f\) is increasing on \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\)
    • If \(\color{black}f'(c) < 0\) for all \(\color{black}c\) in \(\color{black}(a,\ b)\), then \(\color{black}f\) is decreasing on \(\color{black}[a,\ b]\)

    Algebraically finding intervals of increase and decrease

    Let \(\color{black}f\) be a differentiable function on an interval \(\color{black}I\). To find intervals on which \(\color{black}f\) is increasing and decreasing:
    1.) Find the critical values of \(\color{black}f\). That is, find all \(\color{black}c \in I\) where \(\color{black}f'(c)=0\) or \(\color{black}f'\) is not defined. 2.) Use the critical values to divide \(\color{black}I\) into subintervals. 3.) Pick any point \(\color{black}p\) in each subinterval, and find the sign of \(\color{blacl}{f'(p)}\).
    • If \(\color{black}f'(p)>0\), then \(\color{black}f\) is increasing on that subinterval.
    • If \(\color{black}f'(p) < 0\), then \(\color{black}f\) is decreasing on that subinterval.

    • Absolute Extrema: the point at which is the maximum or minimum value of the function over its entire domain.

    Antiderivative

    An antiderivative, inverse derivative, primitive function, primitive integral or indefinite integral of a function \(\color{black}f\) is a differentiable function \(\color{black}F\) whose derivative is equal to the original function f. This can be stated symbolically as F' = f. The process of solving for antiderivatives is called antidifferentiation (or indefinite integration), and its opposite operation is called differentiation, which is the process of finding a derivative. This should not be confused with the term integration. Capital Roman letters such as \(\color{black}F\) and \(\color{black}G\) are often used to defined . The general form of an antiderivative is written using the integral symbol with no bounds \(\color{black}\displaystyle \int f(x) \, dx\) There are infinitely many antiderivatives of a given function \(\color{black}f\). These antiderivatives differ from each other by a constant, called the constant of integration. The symbol \(\color{black}C\) (or \(\color{black}c\)) is typically used to denote this arbitrary constant of integration. The constant of integration is a constant term added to an antiderivative of a function \(\color{black}f(x)\) to indicate that the antiderivative of \(\color{black}f(x)\) (i.e., the set of all antiderivatives of \(\color{black}f(x)\)), is only defined up to an additive constant. The constant expresses an ambiguity inherent in the construction of antiderivatives. More specifically, if \(\color{black}F(x)\) is an antiderivative of a function \(\color{black}f(x)\), then the set of all antiderivatives of \(\color{black}f(x)\) is given by the functions \(\color{black}F(x) + C\), where \(\color{black}C\) is an arbitrary constant (meaning that any value of \(\color{black}C\) would make \(\color{black}F(x)+C\) a valid antiderivative). For that reason, the indefinite integral is often written as \(\displaystyle \int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+C\) The set of all antiderivatives of a given function are vertical translations of each other, with each graph's vertical location depending upon the value \(\color{black}C\). There are many functions whose antiderivatives, even though they exist, cannot be expressed in terms of elementary functions. Such functions are referred to as nonelementary antiderivatives or nonelementary integrals. Even if it is not possible to evaluate an antiderivative in elementary terms, one can always approximate a corresponding definite integral by numerical integration methods such as the Riemann Sums.

    Integral

    Integration, the process of computing an integral, is one of the two fundamental operations of calculus, the other being differentiation. Integration started as a method to solve problems in mathematics and physics, such as calculating areas, volumes, and their generalizations, or determining displacement from velocity. The integrals enumerated in this section are those termed definite integrals, which can be interpreted as the net signed area of a region in the plane that is bounded by the graph of a given function over an interval, or interpreted as the accumulated sum of the function's values changing over time. Besides definite integrals, integrals also refer to the concept of an antiderivative or indefinite integrals, a function whose derivative is the given function. Whether the term "integral" refers to the definite or indefinite integral will vary in context, but will typically refer to the definite integral. Although methods of calculating areas and volumes dated from ancient Greek mathematics, the principles of definite integration were formulated independently by mathematicians Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz in the late 17th century, who thought of the area under a curve as an infinite sum of rectangles of infinitesimal width. Bernhard Riemann later gave a rigorous definition of integrals, which is based on a limiting procedure that approximates the area of a region by breaking the region into infinitesimally thin vertical rectangles. In the early 20th century, Henri Lebesgue generalized Riemann's formulation by introducing what is now referred to as the Lebesgue integral; it is more robust than Riemann's in the sense that a wider class of functions are Lebesgue-integrable.

    Notation

    The modern notation for integration follows from Leibniz's notes; the symbol of integration \( \int\), was based on the Å¿ (elongated s) character and was chosen to suggest summation ("S" in "sum"). In general, the definite integral of a real-valued function \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) with respect to a real variable \(\color{black}x\) on an interval \(\color{black}{[a, b]}\) is written as \(\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x)\ dx\) and is read as "the integral of \(\color{black}f(x)\) with respect to \(\color{black}x\), from \(\color{black}a\) to \(\color{black}b\)", though this may vary slightly. The function \(\color{black}{f(x)}\) is called the integrand, the points \(\color{black}a\) and \(\color{black}b\) are called the limits (or bounds) of integration, and the integral is said to be over the interval \(\color{black}{[a, b]}\), called the interval of integration. The symbol \(\color{black}dx\), called the differential of the variable \(\color{black}x\), indicates that the variable of integration is \(\color{black}x\); it is sufficient to interpret \(\color{black}dx\) as a syntatical device that is paired with an integral expression to indicate the variable of integration —— rather than assigning it some special meaning. A function is said to be integrable if its integral over its domain is finite. When the limits are omitted, as in \(\displaystyle \int f(x)\ dx\) the integral is called an indefinite integral or antiderivative.

    Formal Definitions

    net signed area can be positive, negative, or zero. If the area above the x-axis is larger, the net signed area is positive. If the area below the x-axis is larger, the net signed area is negative. If the areas above and below the x-axis are equal, they cancel out and the net signed area is zero. regions above the x-axis have positive area, while regions below the x-axis have negative area. The value of a definite integral over an interval represents the net signed area under a curve bounded by the function over the forementioned interval, and this area is a geometric quantity that is always a real number. There are many ways of formally defining an definite integral, not all of which are equivalent. The differences exist mostly to deal with differing special cases which may not be integrable under other definitions, but also occasionally for pedagogical reasons. The most commonly used definitions are Riemann integrals and Lebesgue integrals, where the former is typically taught in an introductory-level Calculus course, contrary to the latter. The Riemann integral is the simplest integral to define, and it allows one to integrate every continuous function as well as some not-too-badly discontinuous functions. The Lebesgue integral extends the definition of the integral, allowing one to integrate a much broader class of functions.

    Riemann Integral

    A Riemann sum is a certain kind of approximation of an integral by a finite sum. The Riemann sum is calculated by partitioning the region into shapes (rectangles, trapezoids, parabolas, or cubics) that together form a region that is similar to the region being measured, then calculating the area for each of these shapes, and finally adding all of these small areas together. Because the region by the small shapes is usually not exactly the same shape as the region being measured, the Riemann sum will differ from the area being measured. This error margin can be reduced by dividing up the region more finely, using smaller and smaller shapes. As the shapes get smaller and smaller, the sum approaches the Riemann integral. Loosely speaking, the Riemann integral is the limit of the Riemann sums of a function as the partitions get finer. If the forementioned limit exists then the function is said to be integrable over that interval (or more specifically Riemann-integrable). The Riemann sum can be made as close as desired to the Riemann integral by making the partition fine enough.

    The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

    Main section: The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

    First Part

    If \(\color{black}f\) is a continuous real-valued function defined on a closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\), then the area function \(\color{black}A\) defined as \(\displaystyle A(x) = \int_{a}^x f(t)\ dt,\ \text{ such that}\ x \in [a, b],\) is continuous on \(\color{black}[a, b]\) and differentiable on the open interval \(\color{black}(a, b)\), and satisfies \(\displaystyle A'(x) = f(x)\) for all \(\color{black}x\) in \(\color{black}(a,b)\) so \(\color{black}A\) is an antiderivative of \(\color{black}f\)

    Second Part

    The Second Fundamental Theorem of Calculus relates definite integrals with differentiation and provides a method to compute a definite integral when the antiderivative of its integrand is known. Let \(\color{black}f\) be a real-valued function on a closed interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\) and \(\color{black}F\) a continuous function on \(\color{black}[a, b]\) and an antiderivative of \(\color{black}f\), such that \(\displaystyle \color{black}F'(x) = f(x) \quad \text{ or } \quad F(x) = \int f(x) + C\) If \(\color{black}f\) is Riemann-integrable on \(\color{black}[a, b]\), then \(\displaystyle \int_{a}^b f(x)\ dx = F(b) - F(a)\) stating that the integral of \(\color{black}f\) over the interval \(\color{black}[a, b]\) is equal to [the antiderivative evaluated at \(\color{black}b\)] - [the antiderivative evaluated at \(\color{black}a\)]. For example, if \(\color{black}f(x) = x^2\) and \(\color{black}F = \color{black}\large\frac{x^3 \normalsize}{3}\), then the process of evaluating the definite integral of \(\color{black}f(x)\) on the interval \(\color{black}[0, 8]\) is as follows: \(\displaystyle \begin{align}\int_{0}^8 f(x) dx & = F(b) - F(a) \\ & = \frac{8^3}{3} -\frac{0^3}{3} \\[5px] & = \frac{512}{3} - 0 \\[5px] &= 170\, \frac{2}{3} \end{align}\) So in conclusion, \(\displaystyle \int_{0}^8 f(x) dx = 170\, \frac{2}{3}\) If the antiderivative \(\color{black}F(x)\) of a function \(\color{black}f\) is not continuous, then the second part of the fundamental theorem does not hold. Consider \(\displaystyle \int^{-1}_{-1} \frac{1}{x^2} \, dx\) \(\displaystyle \begin{align} \int^{1}_{-1} \frac{1}{x^2} \, dx & = F(b) - F(a) \\ & = -\frac{1}{x} \biggr]^1_{-1} \\& = -\frac{1}{1} - \left(-\frac{1}{(-1)}\right) \\ & = -2\end{align} \) This is because \(\color{black}f(x)\), the integrand, is not continuous at \(\color{black}x = 0\)

    Improper Integral

    The concept of improper integrals serves the purpose of providing a method to integrate functions that are not integrable in the conventional sense (as a Riemann integral, for instance) due to unbounded domains, singularies, and other kinds of discontinuities. While an improper integral is typically written symbolically just like a standard definite integral, it can be rewritten to represent a limit of a definite integral or a sum of such limits. This is done to extend the definition of integrals that would otherwise, not meet the required circumstances to be integrable (in the sense of Riemann for example). As a consequence, the concept of convergence is introduced, where an improper integral is said to be convergent or to converge if the limit(s) defining it exists(and finite); otherwise, the integral is said to be divergent or to diverge. In the simplest case of a real-valued function of a single variable integrated (in the sense of Riemann or Darboux) over a single interval, an improper integral refers to integrals of the following forms:
    1. \(\color{black}\displaystyle \int _{a}^{\infty }f(x)\,dx\), where \(\color{black}f(x)\) is defined on the interval \(\color{black}\displaystyle [a,\infty )\), in which case the integral is rewritten as (i.e., defined to be) \(\color{black} \displaystyle \lim _{b\to \infty }\int_{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\);
      2. \(\color{black} \displaystyle \int _{a}^{\infty }f(x)\,dx = \lim _{b\to \infty }\int_{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\)
    2. \(\color{black}\displaystyle \int _{-\infty}^{b}f(x)\,dx\), where \(\color{black}f(x)\) is defined on the interval \(\color{black}\displaystyle (-\infty, b]\), in which case the integral is rewritten as (i.e., defined to be) \(\color{black} \displaystyle \lim _{a\to -\infty }\int_{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\);
      3. \(\color{black} \displaystyle \int _{-\infty}^{b}f(x)\,dx = \lim _{a\to -\infty }\int_{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\)
    3. \(\color{black}\displaystyle \int _{-\infty}^{\infty }f(x)\,dx\), where \(\color{black}f(x)\) is defined on the interval \(\color{black}\displaystyle (-\infty, \infty)\), in which case the integral is rewritten as (i.e., defined to be) \(\color{black} \displaystyle \lim _{a\to -\infty }\int_{a}^{c}f(x)\,dx + \lim _{b\to \infty }\int_{c}^{b}f(x)\,dx\);
      4. \(\color{black} \displaystyle \int _{-\infty}^{\infty }f(x)\,dx = \lim _{a\to -\infty }\int_{a}^{c}f(x)\,dx + \lim _{b\to \infty }\int_{c}^{b}f(x)\,dx\) for some arbitrary real number \(\color{black}c\)
    4. \(\color{black}\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\), where \(\color{black}f(x)\) is
      • defined on \(\color{black}[a, b)\) but not at \(\color{black}b\) itself due to a discontinuity; in which case the improper integral is rewritten as
        • \(\displaystyle \color{black} \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \lim_{t \to b^-} \int^t_a f(x) \, dx\)
      • defined on \(\color{black}(a, b]\) but not at \(\color{black}a\) itself due to a discontinuity; in which case the improper integral is rewritten as
        • \(\displaystyle \color{black} \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \lim_{t \to a^+} \int^b_t f(x) \, dx\)
      • defined on \(\color{black}(a, b)\) but neither at \(\color{black}a\) or \(\color{black}b\); in which case the improper integral is rewritten as
        • \(\displaystyle \color{black} \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \lim_{s \to a^+} \int_s^c f(x) \, dx + \lim_{t \to b^-} \int_c^t f(x) \, dx \) where \(\color{black}c\) is some value strictly between \(\color{black}a\) and \(\color{black}b\)
      • defined on \(\color{black}[a, b]\) but has a discontinuity at some \(\color{black}c\) strictly between \(\color{black}a\) and \(\color{black}b\), i.e. where \(\color{black} a < c < b\); in which case the improper integral is rewritten as
        • \(\displaystyle \color{black} \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \lim_{t \to c^-} \int_a^t f(x) \, dx + \lim_{t \to c^+}\int_t^b f(x) \, dx \)
    The first three cases where the intervals are unbounded, cover what are sometimes known as improper integrals of the first type or kind; integrals in case 4 that are improper because of vertical asymptotes are of the "second" type or kind; and cases which combine aspects of both types are sometimes described as being of the "third" kind. A limitation of the technique of improper integration is that the limit of an integral must be taken with respect to only one of its endpoint at a time. Thus, in the third case and last two subcases of the fourth case, the improper integral is redefined as the summation of two seperate limits of two distinct improper integrals of the first kind. These two limits must both exist (be convergent) for the improper integral as a whole to be convergent or to converge. If either of the two limits is divergent, then so is this improper integral. Let's clarify the way the improper integral is rewritten in subcases of the fourth case; the variables \(\color{black}s\) and \(\color{black}t\) tends to the endpoint from the interior of the interval; it might happen that an integrand is discontinuous near an interior point rather than an endpoint, in which case the integral must be split at that point as shown in the fourth subcase

    Mean Value Theorem for Integrals

    Let's define the mean of a function over an interval as a requisite to understand the Mean Value Theorem. The mean of a function \(\color{black}f(x)\) over an interval \(\color{black}I = [a,b]\) is the accumulated sum of the values of \(\color{black}f(x)\) over \(\color{black}I\), divided by the length of \(\color{black}I\). The accumulated sum of the values of \(\color{black}f(x)\) can be found by taking the definite integral of \(\color{black}f(x)\). The length of \(\color{black}I\) can be found by subtracting its upper endpoint by its lower endpoint, i.e. \(\color{black}b - a\). So the mean value of \(\color{black}f(x)\) on an interval \(\color{black}[a,b]\) can be defined as \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{b-a}\int^b_a f(x) \, dx.\) In a way, this is analogous to the arithmetic mean. The arithmetic mean is the accumulated sum of a collection of numbers divided by the count of numbers in the collection.
    (The Mean Value Theorem for Integrals) The theorem states that if a real-valued function is continuous on the closed interval \(\color{black}[a,b]\), then there exists atleast one point \(\color{black}c \in [a, b]\) such that \(\displaystyle \color{black}f(c) = \frac{1}{b-a}\int^b_a f(x) \, dx.\) This definition may be presented with a slight algebraic manipulation, where the equation is re-arranged to isolate the integral expression: \(\displaystyle \color{black}\int^b_a f(x)\, dx = f(c)(b-a)\)

    Series and Sequences

    The position of an element in a sequence is its rank or index. The initial element of a sequence is assigned index 0 or 1, depending on the context or a specific convention. A sequence's length can be finite as in the case of \(\color{black}(1,\, 2,\, 3,\, 4)\), or infinite as in the case of the sequence of positive odd integers \(\color{black}(1,\, 3,\, 5,\, 7,\, \ldots)\) where the ellipsis [\(\color{black}\ldots\)] designates infinity. Sequences should not be confused with sets. A sequence is an enumerated collection of objects in which repetitions and order matters. In a set, order is neither significant or meaningful and number of times an element appears in a set is meaningless For example, (M, A, R, Y) is a sequence of letters with the letter 'M' first and 'Y' last. This sequence differs from (A, R, M, Y), because order matters.

    Definition

    A sequence is formally defined as a function whose domain is an interval of integers. The definition in this section will cover real-valued sequences. Note that the codomain does not necessarily have to be the set of real numbers, as it can also be the set of complex numbers for example. A real-valued sequence is a function whose domain is an interval of integers \(\color{black} \{n_0,\, n_{0+1},\, n_{0+2},\, \ldots\}\) and whose codomain is a subset of the real numbers. \( \color{black}\begin{align} f : \{n_0,\, n_{0+1},\, n_{0+2},\, \ldots & \} \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \\ & n \mapsto f(n) \end{align} \) Authors may use a narrower definition by requiring the domain of a sequence to be the set of natural numbers \(\color{black}\mathbb{N}\). For example, suppose that the domain of a sequence is the set of natural numbers \(\color{black}\mathbb{N}\) and the sequence maps each natural number to two times itself. \( \color{black}\begin{align} f :\,\, & \mathbb{N} \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \\ & n \mapsto 2n \end{align} \) Although sequences are formally defined as a type of function, they are usually distinguished notationally from functions in that the input is written as a subscript rather than in parentheses, that is, \(\color{black}a_n\) rather than \(\color{black}a(n)\). There are terminological differences as well: the value of a sequence at the lowest input (often 1) is called the "first element" of the sequence, the value at the second smallest input (often 2) is called the "second element", etc. Also, while a function abstracted from its input is usually denoted by a single letter, e.g. \(\color{black}f\), a sequence abstracted from its input is usually written by a notation such as \(\color{black}(a_{n})_{n\in S}\), or just as \(\color{black}(a_{n})\). Then for each \(\color{black}n \in S\), \(\color{black}f(n)\) is denoted \(\color{black}a_n\). Here \(\color{black}S\) is the domain, or index set, of the sequence.

    Notation

    The ellipsis \(\color{black}...\) is used when the sequence pattern can be deduced based on the explicitly listed terms of the sequence. There are a number of ways to denote a sequence. One way to denote a finite sequence is to seperate each individual term by a comma, all enclosed in parantheses\(\color{black}()\) or curly brackets\(\color{black}\{\}\). For instance, \(\color{black}(1, 2, 3, 4)\), which can be read as "one, two, three, four," or \(\color{black}(1, 2, 3,\, \ldots, 6)\), which can be read as "one, two, three, and so on until 6." Infinite sequences are composed in a similar way, but with the exception that an ellpsis is explicitly substituted for the terminal segment of the sequence to indicate an unlimited sequence of terms. For instance, the infinite sequence of positive odd integers is written as \(\color{black}(1, 3, 5, 7,\, \ldots)\) Another way is to begin with writing a general formula for computing the \(\color{black}n\)th term as a function of \(\color{black}n\), enclose it in parantheses, curly brackets, or triangular brackets and appending a subscript to indicate the set of values that indexes the sequence. In a case where the indexing set of a sequence is the set of natural numbers \(\color{black}\mathbb{N}\) for example, then the sequence is indexed by non-negative integers. For example, the infinite sequence of even numbers can be denoted as \(\color{black}(2n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\). The infinite sequence of squares can be denoted as \(\color{black}(n^2)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\). Other brackets can be used, such as \(\{n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) or \(\langle n \rangle_{n \in \mathbb{N}}\) This notation is often combined with the technique of treating the terms of a sequence as individual variables. This yields expressions like \(\displaystyle \color{black} (a_n)_{n \mkern 1mu \in \mkern 1mu \mathbb{N}} \), which denotes a sequence whose \(\color{black}n\)th element is given by the variable \(\color{black}a_{n}\). \(\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}a_{1}&=1{\text{st element of }}(a_n)_{n \mkern 1mu \in \mkern 1mu \mathbb{N}}\\a_{2}&=2{\text{nd element of }}(a_n)_{n \mkern 1mu \in \mkern 1mu \mathbb{N}} \\a_{3}&=3{\text{rd element of }}(a_n)_{n \mkern 1mu \in \mkern 1mu \mathbb{N}} \\&\;\;\vdots \\a_{n-1}&=(n-1){\text{th element}}\\a_{n}&=n{\text{th element}}\\a_{n+1}&=(n+1){\text{th element}}\\&\;\;\vdots \end{aligned}}\) An alternative to writing the domain of a sequence in the subscript is to indicate the range of values that the index can take by listing its highest and lowest legal values. \(\color{black}(a_k)^n_{k = i}\) means the list of terms in the sequence \(\color{black}a_k\), ranging from \(\color{black}k = i\) to \(\color{black}k = n\); i.e.: \(\color{black}(a_k)^n_{k = i} = (a_i,\, a_{i + 1}\, a_{i + 2}\, \ldots, a_n)\) For example, \(\color{black}(k^2)^{10}_{k = 1}\) denotes the ten-term sequence of squares \(\color{black}(1, 4, 9, \ldots, 100)\). In cases where the set of indexing numbers is understood, the subscripts and superscripts are often omitted. That is, one simply writes \(\color{black}(a_{n})\) or \(\color{black}\{a_{n}\}\) for an arbitrary sequence. When a sequence is indexed by natural numbers, zero-based indexing or one-based indexing. \(\color{black} (a_n)^\infty_{n = 1} = (a_n)_{n \in \mathbb{N}} = (a_1, a_2, a_3,\, \ldots)\) Any ordered infinite sequence \(\color{black}(a_1,\, a_2,\, a_3,\, \ldots)\) of terms (that is, numbers, functions, or any mathematical object where the operation addition is defined) defines a series, which is the operation of adding the ai one after the other. To emphasize that there are an infinite number of terms, a series may be called an infinite series. Such a series is represented (or denoted) by an expression like \(a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + \cdots\) or in summation notation \(\displaystyle \sum^\infty_{i = 1}a_i\) The symbol, \(\color{black} \sum\), is an enlarged form of the capital Greek letter sigma that compactly represents the summation of terms, defined as \( \displaystyle \sum_{i = m}^n a_i = a_m + a_{m + 1} + a_{m + 2}\, +\, ...\, +\, a_{n - 1} + a_n\) where \(i\) is the index of summation; \(a_i\) is an indexed variable representing each term of the sum; \(m\) is the lower bound of summation, and \(n\) is the upper bound of summation. The "\(i = m\)" under the summation symbol means that the initial index starts out as \(m\). The index, \(i\), is incremented by one for each successive term, stopping when \(i = n\).     Absolute convergence

    An infinite series \(\displaystyle \color{black}\sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n\) is said to converge absolutely or to be absolutely convergent, if the corresponding series \(\displaystyle \color{black}\sum_{n = 0}^\infty |a_n| = |a_1| + |a_2| + |a_3| + \cdots\) or namely, the series whose terms are the absolute values of the original series, converges.     Power series

    One can view a power series as a polynominal of infinite degree, though a distinction should be made between a power series and a polynominal. An infinite series of the form \(\displaystyle \sum_{n = 0}^\infty a_n(x - c)^n = a_0 + a_1(x - c) + a_2(x - c)^2 + \ldots \quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad\quad (1)\) is called a power series in \(\color{black} x - c\) or a power series centered at \(\color{black}c\) or a power series about \(c\), where \(\color{black}a_n\) represents the coefficient of the \(\color{black}n\)th term and \(\color{black}c\) is an arbitrary constant that is said to be the value where the series is centered at. The convergence of the power series \(\color{black}(1)\) is described by one of the following three possible cases:
    • (i) The interval of convergence will always include \(\color{black}x = c\)
    • (ii) The series converges for all \(\color{black}x\)
    • (iii) There is a non-negative number \(\color{black}R\) with \(\color{black}0 < R \leq \infty\) such that the power series \(\color{black}(1)\) converges whenever \(\color{black}|x - c| < R\) and diverges whenever \(\color{black}|x - c| > R\).
    The number \(R\) in case three (iii) is called the radius of convergence of the power series. By convention, the radius of convergence is \(\color{black}R = 0\) in (i) and \(\color{black}R = \infty\) in (ii). The interval of convergence of a power series is the interval that consists of all values of \(\color{black}x\) for which the power series converges. In case (i) this interval of convergence consist of just a single point \(\color{black}c\). In case (ii) this interval is \(\color{black}(-\infty, \infty)\). In case (iii) note that when \(\color{black}x\) is an endpoint of the interval, that is \(\color{black}x = a \pm R\), anything can happen —— the series may or may not \). The interval of convergence can be determined after first determining the radius of convergence. In general, the radius of convergence \(\color{black}R\) can be determined using the Ratio Test or sometimes the Root Test. The Ratio and Root Tests will always yield to be inconclusive if \(\color{black}x\) is an endpoint of the interval of convergence.

    Differential Equations

    A differential equation (abbrev. DE) is an equation that relates one or more unknown functions and their derivatives. Similar to algebraic equations, where you are to find its solutions that fullfill the condition stated by the equation (e.g., 2x + 4 = 8; x = 2), the study of differential equations consists of the study of their solutions (the set of functions that satisfy each equation). Differential equations can be classified into several types. Apart from describing the properties of the equation itself, these classes of differential equations can help inform the choice of approach to a solution. Commonly used distinctions include whether the equation is ordinary or partial, linear or non-linear, and homogeneous or heterogeneous. This list is far from exhaustive; there are many other properties and subclasses of differential equations which can be very useful in specific contexts. The order of the differential equation is the highest order of derivative of the unknown function that appears in the differential equation. For example, an equation containing only first-order derivatives is a first-order differential equation, an equation containing the second-order derivative is a second-order differential equation, and so on.
